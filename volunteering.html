Volunteering

Once you finish orientation, you're ready to volunteer. Get started by signing up for your weekday, letting the coordinator know, and reaching out to your attending. No need to wait for an assignment email.

Reminder of the commitment: the program asks for a total of 100 volunteer hours, in shifts of at least three hours.

How a volunteer day works

  1. The day before

    Text your team's attending on their cell phone (from the attending contact list) to confirm the meeting time and where to meet — meeting spots vary by attending.

  2. Meet your attending

    On arrival — with your volunteer ID badge on — meet the attending to confirm which patients to see and which of the tasks below to focus on.

  3. Spend time at the bedside

    Visit your patients and work through the tasks — communication, compassionate presence, and the 4Ms workflows below.

  4. Report back

    Before you leave, report the outcomes of your visits to the attending.

Can't make your shift? Text your attending and email Andrea Benya (andrea.benya@mssm.edu) as soon as you know.

Safety & boundaries

  • If a patient appears unwell or acutely confused, notify the nursing staff or your team right away — do not handle it yourself.
  • Do not enter rooms requiring N95 masks — volunteers are not fit-tested for them. If you're unsure about a room's precautions, ask the nursing staff first.
  • Do not give medical advice. Pass patients' and families' medical questions to the team.
Full logistics: the AFHS Volunteer Program Protocol covers the program's logistics and procedures in full detail (Word, Mount Sinai login).

What you can help with

Tasks that age-friendly trained volunteers can assist with, organized by the 4Ms. Your attending will tell you which apply to each patient.

Medication

Medication reconciliation

Help patients and families review the medications they actually take, and relay what you learn to the medical team. If a pharmacy call is needed, the team will give you the patient's name and date of birth and the pharmacy phone number.

Med Rec Pocket Card (PDF)
Mobility

Falls education

Provide patients and families with fall-prevention education based on CDC guidelines and suggestions.

In-Hospital Fall Reduction handout (PDF, Sinai login)
Mentation

Non-pharmacological delirium prevention

For patients at high risk of hospital delirium: help with family delirium education and prevention — reorienting the patient, lighting, signage, supporting sensory deficits (hearing aids, glasses), the sleep–wake cycle, and activities.

What Families Need to Know (PDF) Delirium Guide for Patients & Families (PDF)
What Matters

Health Care Proxy (HCP)

For patients identified with the team on rounds: help complete the HCP form and serve as one of the two witnesses. Give the completed form to the medical team (attending, fellow, resident, or PA) for confirmation as the second witness. The team will brief you on the patient's background first.

NYS Health Care Proxy form (PDF)
What Matters

My Story

With most patients: learn their hobbies and interests, sources of strength, and important values, using the My Story document (Appendix B of the training guide). Spiritual Care services are also available at MSM.

Training guide incl. My Story (PDF, Sinai login)
