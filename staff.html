For program staff

Staff guide

For the Administrative Coordinator, Orientation Trainers, and MD / Volunteer Lead: the onboarding workflow at a glance, and what this site automates at each step.

Step Responsible What to do Site support
1. Commitment attestation Candidate (self-serve) The candidate attests online to the 100-hour commitment and 3+ hour shifts, and gives their preferred site and weekday availability — replaces the screening interview. Attestation form is linked on the Home page; responses appear in the form's Responses tab, with an email notification per submission.
2. MD / Lead review MD / Volunteer Lead Meet the candidate, assess interpersonal fit, flag concerns, and approve to move forward. Before accepting, check the candidate's weekday availability (from the attestation) against the sign-up list so new volunteers land on open days.
3. Pre-orientation education Administrative Coordinator Set up a Sinai email if needed, then send the volunteer the link to the Pre-Orientation page. (MSQ volunteers identified by Dr. Kamal skip steps 1–2 and start here.) The Pre-Orientation page IS the packet — surveys, videos, CAPC modules, and reading in one link.
4. Verify completion Administrative Coordinator Check the completion-form spreadsheet, review, and connect the volunteer with an Orientation Trainer. Completion-form responses appear in the form's Responses tab (Microsoft Forms) for the form owner.
5. Orientation training Orientation Trainers One Zoom session with role-playing + about three in-person sessions. Assess communication, professionalism, compassionate presence, role-specific skills, and readiness to volunteer independently. The Orientation page pre-briefs volunteers on the format and assessment criteria.
6. Orientation completion Orientation Trainers Have the volunteer complete the AFHS Volunteer Confidence Survey as the last step, then notify the MD / Volunteer Lead that they can be placed. Survey is linked on the Orientation page. Anonymous; used to correlate volunteer self-confidence with trainer evaluations. Temporary — revisit after summer 2026 once we know which parts of orientation are most helpful.
7. Sign up & start volunteering Volunteer (self-serve) After orientation, point the volunteer to the Volunteering page. They sign up for a weekday and contact their attending directly — no assignment email needed. Volunteers sign up and find contacts via the Volunteering page. Staff can see who has signed up on the sign-up list (Mount Sinai login). Optional welcome/nudge: email template below.
8. Program completion Administrative Coordinator After the volunteer's 100 hours, confirm they take the program post-survey and close out their participation. Post-survey is linked on the Volunteering page; responses appear in the form's Responses tab (Google Forms).

Volunteer tasks — physician reference

What volunteers can do and what physicians should provide for each task. Click a task to expand.

Safety: volunteers are not permitted in rooms requiring N95 masks, as they are not fit-tested for them. Physicians should also consider volunteer comfortability for patient rooms under contact precautions.
My Story What Matters
Volunteers can
  • Identify hobbies/interests, sources of strength, important values, and what matters most to patients
  • Speak to and listen to patients
Physicians should
  • Provide a list of any/all alert patients with room numbers

Applicable to all alert patients. Lower priority than the tasks below, but emphasize it when the patient list is short or few other tasks need assistance.

Obtaining a medication list Medication
Volunteers can
  • Obtain and cross-reference a medication list from the patient, family, or pharmacy
Physicians should
  • Identify patients with unconfirmed medication lists and provide name / DOB / pharmacy phone number (as needed)
Health Care Proxy (HCP) What Matters
Volunteers can
  • Explain the importance of the HCP form
  • Assist with HCP form completion and serve as a witness (1 of 2); give the form(s) to the attending, fellow, or resident for confirmation
Physicians should
  • Identify patients who need HCPs, provide any necessary background information, and serve as the HCP witness (2 of 2)
Falls education Mobility
Volunteers can
  • Provide in-hospital / at-home falls education (CDC guidelines)
  • Assist in understanding fall history, mobility status, and home environment
  • Emphasize specific items as needed (durable medical equipment, asking for help, etc.)
Physicians should
  • Identify patients / family members admitted for falls or at fall risk
Non-pharmacological delirium prevention Mentation
Volunteers can
  • Assist with family delirium education
  • Engage in delirium prevention: patient orientation, lighting, signage, supporting sensory deficits (hearing aids / glasses), the sleep–wake cycle, and activities
Physicians should
  • Identify confused or at-risk patients
